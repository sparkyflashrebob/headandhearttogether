Imagine you are drifting quietly in your boat on the calm waters of Corte Madera Creek. Suddenly, out of nowhere, another vessel crashes straight into yours.

If you look up and see someone at the helm, you instantly flare up with rage, blame, and indignation. How could they be so careless?

But if you look over and realize the boat is completely empty—that it simply broke loose from a dock upstream—what happens to your anger? It vanishes in a flash. You don't scream at an empty boat. You simply recognize it as nature unfolding, and you skillfully adjust.

The secret to true inner peace isn't forcing the rest of the world to steer perfectly; it’s learning how to empty our own boat.

The Heavy Stones We Carry

Most of our daily exhaustion doesn't come from reality itself—it comes from the rigid expectations, defensive armor, and subjective storylines we wrap around it. We let the internal "committee" run the show, constantly oscillating between two extremes:

  • Banging our heads against the wall trying to force outcomes we can't control.

  • Punting and withdrawing from discomfort out of pure avoidance.

When we lack true self-honesty, we end up carrying the heavy stones of pride, memory, and validation-seeking. We might even find ourselves "right on the facts, but wrong in Dhamma"—using truth as a weapon rather than a bridge.
