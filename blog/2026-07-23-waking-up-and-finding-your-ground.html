Waking Up and Finding Your Ground

When Ajahn Chah and his monastics walked their morning alms round through the village paths of Thailand, they would often pass massive boulders resting along the dirt[cite: 1, 5]. At one time Ajahn Chah pointed to a heavy rock and ask the monks if it was heavy. When they agreed it was, he offered a direct truth: the rock is only heavy if you reach down and pick it up. Sitting on the earth, it causes no one any strain.

Life presents plenty of heavy circumstances. We double our own suffering the moment we reach out mentally, grab hold of those situations, and insist they should be different.

Leaving the rock on the ground requires right understing. Grounding itself does not break our default thinking loops—mindfulness does. Being Mindful gives us a chance to wake up. It is the recognition that the mind is darting around, grabbing hold of anger, or getting lost in old stories.

Once mindfulness hits the brakes on that automated cycle, grounding can become our intentional, skillful choice. Grounding is an active, resourceful practice. It is not about ignoring difficulty, turning our backs on problems, or bypassing life. It is choosing to place attention on physical anchors or visualiziung resources that offer genuine stability, safety, and physical ease while we encounter the experinces of life.

There are four key qualities of mindfulness to guide us:

  • Ardent: Bringing a steady, honest effort to stay present throughout the day.

  • Alert: Paying clear attention to where your mind lands from moment to moment.

  • Mindful: Remembering to return to present awareness while also remembering past lessons—knowing what was unskillful and caused pain, and what was skillful and brought peace.

  • Clearly Knowing: Seeing the bare facts in the present moment without adding judgmental commentary, personal drama, or extra stories.

When stress, physical pain, or conflict hits, our usually default mode takes over. Mindfulness can act as the wake-up call. Once awake, you can intentionally select the best method forward: which can include a resourceful grounding anchor that works for you if need be.

You can ground yourself using simple internal touchpoints. Feel gravity holding your feet firmly against the floor, or feel the weight of your seat in the chair. Or follow a steady breath moving through your body. You can also use external resources: walking in a forest or actully touching a tree, feeling your barefoot contact with natural soil, breathing fresh air, viewing or listening to the ocean waves on the shore, or listen to birds on a trail.

Traditional indigenous wisdom reminds us that a mountain does not struggle to be a mountain; it simply stands. When mindfulness wakes you up, you can put the heavy rock down and rest on solid ground.
